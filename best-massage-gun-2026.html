How We Test Massage Guns

Every massage gun on this list went through our testing protocol:

  • Amplitude measurement: We use digital calipers to measure actual head travel distance, not just the manufacturer's claim.
  • Stall force testing: We press each gun against a digital scale until the motor stalls, recording the force in pounds.
  • Noise measurement: Decibel readings at 1 foot distance on each speed setting.
  • Battery life: Run continuously on speed 3 until dead, timing the duration.
  • Real-world use: Each gun gets 2+ weeks of daily use by our testing team (mix of athletes, office workers, and a licensed massage therapist).

Frequently Asked Questions

Are massage guns worth it?

Yes, for most people. Research shows percussion therapy reduces muscle soreness (DOMS) by 30-50% and increases range of motion. They're not a replacement for professional massage, but for daily maintenance and post-workout recovery, they're highly effective and pay for themselves quickly vs. regular massage appointments.

How often should you use a massage gun?

Most experts recommend 1-2 minutes per muscle group, up to 2-3 times daily. Don't exceed 2 minutes on any single spot to avoid bruising or irritation. Use before workouts to warm up muscles and increase blood flow, or after to reduce soreness and speed recovery.

What's the difference between amplitude and stall force?

Amplitude is how deep the head travels (measured in mm) — higher means deeper tissue penetration. Think of it as "depth." Stall force is how much pressure you can apply before the motor stalls — higher means it won't stop when you press hard into dense muscle. Think of it as "power." Both matter, but amplitude is more important for deep tissue work.

Can massage guns be harmful?

When used correctly, no. Avoid using on bones, joints, nerves, bruises, or inflamed areas. Don't use on the neck/throat area or directly over the spine. Never use on open wounds or skin conditions. If you have a medical condition (blood clots, osteoporosis, nerve disorders), consult your doctor first.

Do I need a $400 massage gun?

Not necessarily. If you're a casual user dealing with everyday tension, a $100-200 gun like the Ekrin B37S will serve you well. The $300-400 range is for serious athletes, people with chronic pain, or anyone who values the deepest possible percussion and premium build quality.
